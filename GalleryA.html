HEAVEN ELEVEN

A classic 7-Eleven store in East Vancouver is no more. All the signage came down and plywood went up over the windows at the beginning of March 2023 in the heart of the Collingwood neighbourhood at Kingsway and Joyce.

Farewell Kingsway konbini.

Just 10 days later, Masatoshi Ito, the business leader who took the 7-Eleven brand to Japan and expanded it globally on a massive scale, passed away at age 98.

I captured some photos on a warm evening the previous summer using my trusty Asahi Pentax K1000 35 mm SLR loaded with consumer grade Fuji film. This was an appropriately utilitarian combination of camera and film for a store catering to the masses. And surely this 7-Eleven provided a valuable community service to many on a night that was hot by Vancouver standards, serving up ice-cold Slurpees and salt-replenishing snacks to the air-conditioning deprived and those just revelling in precious summer weather.

The store was optimally situated at 45 degrees on a well-travelled corner unimpeded by gas pumps or any other business pretense. The windows faced the world as a wide welcoming smile, a well-lit stage or an ultra-widescreen back-lit 3D display. Benches sit just across the street on the periphery of the historic Sir Guy Carleton school house. Ample parking was provided as if in recognition of Kingsway’s long history as a motorway into the city. Other remnants of this motoring past still linger but are quickly disappearing as redevelopment races on.

The south-east corner of the Kingsway-Joyce intersection now seems desolate and lifeless. Soon, the presentation centre of a new condominium project will possess the building with slick marketing impervious to exorcisms of any kind. A deep crater in the ground will follow together with dusty, impeded sidewalks, a towering crane, and months of busy noise disrupting even the sound of traffic.

One soldier of a corporate giant has been downed to be replaced by a different kind of business entity. Perhaps a faltering economy could slow down this transformation but there is no bringing the 7-Eleven back from the dead now. The opportunity has arrived for other neighbourhood shops to fill the void with bubble tea, iced coffee and a sure abundance of snacking variety but without the advantage of corner-lot sociability.

Please enjoy this little collection of photos in celebration of a summer mood now lost to history. I have some more film of the aftermath which is yet to be seen and I may find something good enough for showing in the near future for a proper mourning.

Vancouver, April 2023
